Part 2: Electromyography (EMG) Testing Experiment Log

1. Experimental Objective

To use an electromyography (EMG) sensor to verify changes in muscle status (from tension to relaxation/activation) in the upper trapezius during three desk-based relaxation movements, and to document the entire experimental process, including sensor exploration, troubleshooting, and data interpretation.

2. Equipment and Materials

  1. Arduino Uno/Nano microcontroller
  2. EMG muscle sensor module
  3. Three electrodes (positive, negative, reference)
  4. Dupont wires, USB cable
  5. Arduino IDE
  6. Laptop for data recording

3. Sensor Exploration and Electrode Placement

3.1 Initial Attempts and Learning

Initially, electrodes were placed on tendons or bony areas, resulting in near-zero signals or significant noise. After consulting relevant resources, it was determined that electrodes must be placed on the muscle belly (the fleshy, contractile portion of the muscle) to obtain clear EMG signals.

3.2 Final Placement (Upper Trapezius)

  1. Positive electrode: placed on the muscle belly of the upper trapezius (at the junction of the neck and shoulder, where the muscle bulges)
  2. Negative electrode: placed 2–3 cm below the positive electrode, on the same muscle
  3. Reference electrode: placed on a bony prominence (e.g., clavicle or sternum), away from the target muscle, to reduce interference

3.3 Skin Preparation

The skin areas where the electrodes were to be placed were cleaned with alcohol wipes to remove surface oils and dead skin cells, ensuring good electrode-skin contact and minimizing signal interference.

4. Calibration Method

  1. Resting Baseline Calibration: The participant was instructed to fully relax their shoulders and remain still while data were collected for 30 seconds, establishing a baseline for muscle relaxation.
  2. Zero Reference Setting: The average of the resting baseline was used as the zero reference for all subsequent measurements to offset errors caused by sensor drift.

5. Failures and Adjustments

5.1 Port Selection and Upload Failure

  1. Issue: An error appeared when trying to upload the code to the Arduino board.
  2. Possible causes: Wrong port selected (e.g., Bluetooth instead of USB); Board not properly connected or USB cable faulty; Board model in the IDE did not match the actual board
  3. Adjustment: Rechecked the connection and replaced the USB cable; Confirmed the board model in the IDE settings; Reselected the correct serial port
  4. After these steps, the code uploaded successfully.

5.2 Sudden Large Signal Jumps

  1. Issue: The EMG signal suddenly jumped to over 200 units, well above the normal range.
  2. Possible causes: Loose electrodes or a sudden movement by the participant.
  3. Adjustment: Pressed the electrodes down to ensure good contact and reminded the participant to stay still and breathe steadily during the test.

5.3 Baseline Drift

  1. Issue: The baseline signal (orange line) drifted even when the participant stayed still.
  2. Possible causes: Sweating, electrode issues, or interference from nearby electronics.
  3. Adjustment: Dried the skin and moved away potential sources of interference.

5.4 Motion Capture Speed and Code Delay Settings

  1. Issue: The signal couldn't keep up with the movement, causing incomplete or lagging data.
  2. Possible cause: The delay setting in the code was too short, making the sampling rate too fast for the setup.
  3. Adjustment: Increased the delay to set the sampling interval to 100 milliseconds, which helped stabilize the signal during movement.

6. Data Interpretation

Figure 1: Multi-Directional Neck Stretch

  1. Blue line: Raw EMG signal from the upper trapezius
  2. Orange line: Filtered signal (or reference from middle/lower trapezius)
  3. Interpretation: The signal started low at rest, rose with contraction (e.g., shoulder elevation), and dropped during the stretch, reflecting relaxation of the upper trapezius.

Figure 2: Shoulder Rolls

Interpretation: Regular peaks in the blue line matched the rhythm of contraction and relaxation during the movement. The orange line showed the overall trend. After the movement, the signal returned to a level below the starting baseline.

Figure 3: Overhead Stretch with Hands Clasped and Head Tilted Back

Interpretation: An initial peak appeared during arm elevation. During the head tilt, the signal stayed low and stable. Small fluctuations at the end were likely due to returning to a neutral posture.

7. Conclusion and Reflection

7.1 Conclusion

The EMG data successfully validated that all three relaxation movements effectively relaxed the upper trapezius, as evidenced by a significant decrease in EMG signal amplitude during the execution of the movements compared to the tense baseline.

7.2 Reflection and Areas for Improvement

  1. Use more secure electrodes to prevent poor contact issues during testing.
  2. Incorporate more advanced filtering procedures to further reduce signal noise and baseline drift.
  3. Simultaneously collect EMG signals from both the upper trapezius and the middle/lower trapezius to more clearly illustrate the activation patterns of antagonist muscles.

8. Design Implications for Curriculum Development

New Section Added in revision: Translating experiment failures into curriculum design recommendations. This section connects each technical problem we encountered during the experiment to a concrete, actionable design decision for the student body lab curriculum. It embodies the course principle that curriculum design should be informed by authentic learner experience.

Every technical problem recorded in this experiment log carries a curriculum design implication. The table below translates each failure into a concrete recommendation for the student body lab.

Experiment FailureDesign Implication
Port selection and upload failure (5.1)
We spent nearly an hour troubleshooting code upload because the wrong port was selected and the board model didn't match.		 Pre-class equipment checklist: Before the lab, every Arduino board should be pre-tested with the same USB cable that will be used in class. Each station should have a small label indicating the correct port name and board model. A one-page visual troubleshooting card (with screenshots of common error messages and their fixes) should be placed at each station. This prevents the most frustrating kind of delay—the kind where students don't even know what they're looking at.
Electrode placement on wrong tissue (3.1)
We initially placed electrodes on tendons and bony areas, getting near-zero or noisy signals. We only learned through trial and error that the muscle belly is essential.		 Just-in-time anatomy mini-lesson before sensor setup: Rather than letting students discover this through frustration, the teacher should spend five minutes explaining why the muscle belly generates a detectable electrical signal while tendons do not. A simple analogy helps: imagine trying to hear a conversation by pressing your ear against a wall versus against the door. The wall (tendon) transmits almost nothing; the door (muscle belly) lets the sound through. This small conceptual investment at the start saves enormous frustration later.
Sudden large signal jumps (5.2)
Signal jumped to over 200 units when electrodes became loose or the participant made an abrupt movement.		 Post-setup signal check protocol: After placing electrodes, each group should perform three quick shoulder shrugs while watching the Serial Plotter. If the signal spikes cleanly with each shrug and returns to baseline between them, the setup is good. If it jumps erratically or stays flat, the electrodes need rechecking. This protocol takes thirty seconds and catches most placement problems before data collection begins.
Baseline drift (5.3)
Even when the participant stayed still, the baseline slowly drifted, likely due to sweating or environmental interference.		 Environmental setup checklist and re-calibration reminder: The teacher should remind students to keep electronic devices (especially phones) at least one meter away from the Arduino board. If baseline drift exceeds a visible threshold during an experiment, the group should pause, dry the skin, and re-establish the baseline before continuing. Normalizing this as a routine maintenance step (rather than a sign of failure) reduces student anxiety.
Signal lag during fast movement (5.4)
The sampling rate was too fast for smooth movement capture, causing jagged, incomplete data.		 Pre-set code template with annotated delay values: Provide students with working code that already has the delay set to 100 milliseconds, along with a comment explaining what the delay does and how changing it affects the signal. This gives students a stable starting point while still leaving room for curious groups to experiment with different settings.

Across all five failures, a common pattern emerges: the most significant barriers were not conceptual but logistical. Students understood the science quickly once the equipment was working. The curriculum should therefore front-load technical setup support while keeping the biological inquiry at the center of the experience. Every minute saved on troubleshooting is a minute gained for genuine scientific discovery.

4. How Student Experience Shaped This Curriculum

New Section Added in revision: Connecting design journals to curriculum decisions. This section directly quotes the three design journals to justify the scaffolding choices above. Rather than selecting scaffolding strategies from generic pedagogical principles, this curriculum is shaped by the actual emotional and cognitive trajectory that we as students experienced. This reflects the course emphasis on learner-centered design informed by authentic experience data.

The scaffolding choices in the table above are not random selections from a teaching methods textbook. Each one is a direct response to a specific moment of confusion, frustration, or discovery recorded in our design journals. Here is where those choices came from.

The first fifteen minutes are the most fragile. Mike wrote that he panicked when he saw the Arduino board and all the wires, and that the group spent the first hour just trying to get the code to upload. I stood by, handing them parts, feeling like I had nothing to contribute. Yuandi kept thinking, when do we actually get to the muscles? This pattern suggests that the opening of the lab is a high-risk moment. If students spend too long wrestling with equipment they don't understand, the emotional momentum for scientific discovery drains away before it has a chance to build. This is why the curriculum places the pre-tested equipment checklist and the visual troubleshooting card at the very start. The goal is not to eliminate all technical difficulty, but to compress the disorienting phase into a manageable window so that students can reach their first real-time signal before frustration turns into disengagement.

The electrode placement puzzle needs a conceptual answer, not just a procedural one. Yuandi described placing electrodes in three different spots before getting a clean signal, and said she wished someone had explained why the muscle belly works and why tendons don't, rather than leaving her to trial and error. This is a small but telling point. Trial and error can be productive when students have a framework to interpret what they are seeing. Without that framework, it is just blind guessing. The just-in-time mini-lesson on muscle anatomy, placed right before the sensor setup, is designed to give students exactly that framework. It takes five minutes but transforms the electrode placement task from a frustrating guessing game into a testable prediction: if I place the electrode here, I should get a strong signal because this is where the muscle fibers are thickest.

The real-time feedback moment is the engine of the whole experience. All three of us independently described the same turning point: the moment when we moved our shoulders and saw the graph respond instantly. I wrote that it felt like a conversation between my body and the screen. Mike said it felt like a game. Yuandi said that was when science actually happened. This convergence is the strongest design evidence in the entire set of journals. It tells us that the real-time EMG visualization is not just a data collection tool; it is the motivational core of the activity. Everything in the curriculum should be organized to protect and amplify this moment. The technical setup exists to get students there. The data reflection exists to help them make sense of what they saw. But the curriculum should never let these surrounding structures bury the core experience under layers of procedure.

Repetition has a cost that needs to be acknowledged. I wrote that by the fifth trial I was definitely less relaxed than the first, and that I caught myself thinking just get this over with. Yuandi said she got impatient by the third or fourth round. This is a real physiological and motivational problem: the very thing the experiment is measuring (muscle relaxation) can be undermined by the experimental procedure itself (repetitive trials). The curriculum addresses this in two ways. First, the modified task option allows groups that are clearly fatigued to test any exercise once and then refine based on that single trial, rather than forcing multiple repetitions. Second, the teacher is encouraged to normalize this phenomenon by saying something like, if you find yourself getting stiffer with each repetition, that's not a mistake, that's actually more data about how your body responds to repeated demands. This reframes a frustrating experience as an additional scientific observation.

The body knows more than the mind thinks it does. My own most surprising discovery was that the EMG sensor was more honest than my own bodily sensations. I thought I was relaxed, but the baseline signal showed residual activity, meaning my muscle hadn't fully returned to a resting state. This gap between perceived relaxation and actual muscle activity is not just a personal anecdote. It is exactly the kind of conceptual insight that a body lab should be designed to produce. The curriculum's data reflection template explicitly asks students to compare what they expected to see with what the graph actually showed, and to write about any surprises. That question is there because of this specific moment in my own learning.

Open questions are curricular resources, not loose ends. Mike ended his journal wondering how this would work in a real classroom with thirty students. Yuandi wondered whether she was actually measuring what she thought she was measuring. I wondered whether the exercises we designed would work for people with different body types or tension habits. These questions are not failures of the activity to provide closure. They are the beginning of scientific thinking. A well-designed curriculum does not answer every question. Instead, it makes space for these open questions at the end, treating them as evidence that students are thinking beyond the immediate task. The final class discussion in the curriculum outline is designed to surface exactly these kinds of questions and validate them as legitimate scientific inquiry, not as loose ends to be tied up.

Part 5: Design Journal

Design Journal 1: Fan Aoyu (Mike)

When I saw the Arduino board and all the wires at the start of this project, I panicked a little. We spent the first hour just trying to get the code to upload, and I kept getting some error message with no idea what it meant. We tried different USB ports, restarted the computer, checked the board settings—nothing worked until someone suggested we change the cable. That finally fixed the hardware issue, but then we ran into software problems with the Arduino environment. Eventually we figured out that we had to select the right board model inside the IDE as well. Looking back, what I wish someone had told me is that the tech setup should be as simple as possible for a biology lab. Students don't want to spend an hour troubleshooting computers when they signed up to learn about muscles. A pre-checked station or a very clear, step-by-step wiring diagram would have saved us so much time and frustration.

Once we finally got past all that, the science clicked immediately. When we got a signal and I saw the graph move with my shoulder shrug—raising my shoulders made the blue line go up, dropping them made it go down—it all made sense. Muscle contraction equals electrical signal, and I could see it happening in real time. That real-time feedback was super motivating; it felt like a game, trying to make the line go higher or lower.

I arrived assuming that once we had the sensor working, it would just work consistently, but that turned out to be wrong. The signal drifted, and different movements gave us different quality data—it was much more finicky than I expected.

At the end, I'm left wondering how this would actually work in a real classroom with thirty students. Who sets up all the sensors? What happens when half the groups can't get a signal? I don't know whether we can manage that without TA or colleague support, and that question stays with me.

Design Journal 2: Yang Yuandi

I was really excited about the biology side of things, but the beginning ended up being all about wires and code. I kept thinking to myself, when do we actually get to the muscles? The electrode placement was also a bit of a puzzle. We put them where we thought they should go, but got no signal. We moved them a little, got a messy, noisy signal. We moved them again, and finally got a clean one. I wish someone had just explained from the start why the muscle belly picks up the signal and why tendons don't. It would have saved us a whole lot of trial and error. Looking back, I can see that all that fiddling with wires was necessary, but at the time it felt like a detour from what I actually wanted to be doing.

It really clicked when we started comparing the graphs for different movements. The shoulder rolls gave these rhythmic peaks and valleys, almost like waves. The overhead stretch, on the other hand, gave a long, flat, low line. That's when I realized that relaxation isn't just one single state—there are actually different patterns to it. Some movements create this flowing, wave-like pattern, while others just drop the activity and hold it there. Seeing that difference helped me understand the physiology in a much deeper way than just reading about it. It made me realize that the body is way more nuanced than I had given it credit for.

What kept me going was seeing our hypotheses get confirmed. We'd predict that a certain movement would relax the muscle, and then the graph would show exactly that. It felt like we were doing real science, not just following instructions. There was something satisfying about saying "this is what I think will happen" and then watching the screen prove you right. The tedious part was definitely the skin prep and calibration. Wiping with alcohol, waiting for the baseline to settle, sticking and re-sticking electrodes—it was repetitive and seemed to take forever. By the third or fourth round, I found myself getting impatient, just wanting to get to the actual testing part.

I came in assuming that relaxation would mean the signal goes all the way down to zero. But it doesn't. Even when a muscle is relaxed, there's still some baseline activity. I learned that the goal wasn't to hit zero, but to get below the tense baseline. That was a more nuanced understanding than what I started with. It made me realize that my body is always doing something, even when I think it's doing nothing.

We learned a fair amount about the electrical activity of muscles, but I think it would have helped even more if we had a better grasp of muscle anatomy. Understanding exactly which muscles are doing what, and where they're located, would probably deepen our understanding even further. I kept wondering during the experiments whether we were actually measuring what we thought we were measuring, and a stronger anatomy foundation would have given me more confidence there.

Design Journal 3: Lin Jiayuan (Kelly)

At the very beginning, I worked with Mike and Yuandi on Arduino, but I honestly felt quite lost. I wanted to help yet had no idea where to start. They both picked it up faster than I did, so I just stood by, handing them parts occasionally. I felt anxious but couldn't really contribute. As the experiment progressed, however, I gradually realized that seemingly trivial details—how I breathed, how I sat, how I positioned my body—all affected the data. If I had known about this earlier, I would have paid much closer attention and consciously controlled my state from the start, instead of only realizing it when reviewing the data later.

What truly made me connect with the scientific aspect was when I performed shoulder circles while watching the screen. It felt amazing in that moment: every time I lifted my shoulder, the curve rose; when I rotated it back, the curve dropped. There was an unexpectedly direct link between my physical movements and the graphics on the screen. Later, I tried moving faster and slower to see the differences. At a quick pace, the graph became highly jagged, with sharp ups and downs; when I slowed down, the curve turned smooth, like a gentle wave. In that instant, I realized the graph didn't just show that I was moving—it also revealed how I was moving: whether it was rapid or calm, tense or relaxed. This discovery was really fascinating and suddenly made all this feel personal to me, no longer just cold equipment and numbers.

The most enjoyable part of the whole process was the real-time feedback. Every movement I made changed the line on the screen instantly, which was highly intuitive, almost like playing a game—trying to control the line with my body, making it higher, lower, or smoother. I found myself unconsciously wondering: How will it react if I do this? Would it be better if I adjust that way? I got quite hooked on this feeling of conversing between my body and the visuals.

What was tedious, though, was repeating the same movements over and over. To obtain stable data, we performed identical actions repeatedly. By the fifth trial, I was definitely less relaxed than the first; instead, I became somewhat stiff, even thinking, "Just get this over with." Waiting for the baseline to stabilize was also time-consuming. I had to sit still without talking, watching the line on the screen gradually even out, which was honestly quite frustrating. I understand this was necessary to ensure data quality, but it still felt dull in practice.

I used to think I knew best whether my body was relaxed. But the electromyography (EMG) showed me that sometimes when I thought I was relaxed, the baseline signal still remained, meaning my body hadn't fully returned to a resting state. The sensor was more honest than my own bodily sensations, making me rethink my understanding of my body—it turned out there was a gap between what I perceived as relaxed and my body's actual state.

There are still some questions I haven't figured out. I'm curious: if other students did this experiment, would they also find the initial part discouraging? We had several people exploring together and discussing with each other, but if someone faced all this equipment and software alone, would they just give up? Besides, the movement exercises we designed were based on me, tailored to my physical condition and movement habits. Would this setup work for someone with a different body type or different tension habits? I think figuring out how to make this equipment and method more accessible and user-friendly for people with different body types is something worth exploring further.

Looking Back: Re-reading My Own Design Journal

New Section Added in revision: A retrospective reflection on my own journal entry. This short reflection models the metacognitive practice that the course has been encouraging throughout the semester: returning to earlier work with new eyes and identifying what has changed in one's own thinking.

Re-reading this journal now, several months after writing it, I notice things I couldn't have articulated at the time. The moment I described as feeling lost while Mike and Yuandi handled the Arduino was not just a personal shortcoming. It was my first real encounter with a problem that now shapes how I think about designing group labs: when the technical barrier is too high at the start, it doesn't just slow down the group. It sorts people into those who can participate and those who stand by waiting. I didn't feel excluded because anyone excluded me. I felt excluded because I didn't have the entry-level knowledge to even know what to ask. That experience is now one of the strongest arguments I would make for why a body lab curriculum needs to front-load technical accessibility. Not because students can't eventually figure it out, but because every minute spent in confused silence is a minute in which someone is quietly deciding this isn't for them.

I also notice now that my most powerful learning moment was not when I understood a concept, but when I felt a connection between my own body and the visualization on the screen. The graph didn't teach me what muscle contraction is. I already knew that from textbooks. What it taught me was that my body is telling a story I cannot fully perceive through internal sensation alone, and that a sensor can reveal dimensions of that story that my own nervous system filters out. This is, I now realize, a much deeper lesson about the relationship between technology and self-knowledge than anything I expected to learn from sticking electrodes to my shoulder. It also explains why I have been so drawn to designing learning experiences around real-time feedback throughout the rest of this course. That moment of watching the curve respond to my movement was, for me, the emotional anchor that made the entire activity feel meaningful.

The open questions at the end of my journal still feel genuine to me. I still don't know whether this activity would work equally well for someone with a very different body or a very different relationship to physical movement. If anything, the semester has made me more aware of how much variability there is between learners, and how much of curriculum design is not about finding the one right version but about building enough flexibility into the structure that different people can find their own way into the experience. That question about accessibility, which I posed tentatively at the end of this journal, has become one of the central concerns I carry into my thinking about STEM education more broadly.
